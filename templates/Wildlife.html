Rwanda's Wildlife

Rwanda is a haven for wildlife enthusiasts, offering unforgettable encounters with some of the world's most remarkable species. The star attraction is the endangered mountain gorilla, found in the lush Volcanoes National Park, where you can trek through misty forests to observe these gentle giants up close. Nyungwe Forest National Park is home to 13 primate species, including chimpanzees and colobus monkeys, as well as hundreds of bird and butterfly species. In Akagera National Park, explore sprawling savannas teeming with Africa’s iconic wildlife—lions, elephants, giraffes, zebras, and buffalos—all set against stunning lake and mountain backdrops. Rwanda’s rich biodiversity makes it a paradise for nature lovers and an unforgettable destination for wildlife adventures.

Places to Visit in Rwanda's Wildlife

Akagera National Park

Akagera National Park, sprawled across 1,122 square kilometers in eastern Rwanda, is a revitalized savanna gem where visitors can experience classic African safaris amidst golden plains, acacia woodlands, and a chain of shimmering lakes like Ihema. Once diminished by poaching, this biodiversity hotspot has roared back to life since the 2010s through a partnership with African Parks, reintroducing lions in 2015 and rhinos in 2017 to join resident elephants, buffalo, and elusive leopards—completing the Big Five. Guests embark on thrilling game drives in open-top 4x4s, spotting giraffes, zebras, and over 480 bird species like the shoebill stork, while boat safaris reveal hippos and crocodiles; night drives uncover hyenas, all framed by Rwanda’s conservation success story, supported by anti-poaching patrols and community revenue, with lodging options from luxury tented camps to budget sites just 2.5 hours from Kigali.

Nyungwe Forest National Park

Nyungwe Forest National Park, a 1,019-square-kilometer montane rainforest in southern Rwanda, is an ancient, misty wilderness dating to the Ice Age, offering a biodiversity paradise of over 1,068 plant species, 13 primate types—including chimpanzees—and 300+ bird species like the great blue turaco. A UNESCO Biosphere Reserve contender, it invites visitors to trek at dawn through dense trails to observe playful chimps, traverse the 60-meter-high Canopy Walkway for treetop vistas, or hike to Isumo Waterfall, with night walks revealing bushbabies and serval cats. Conservation thrives here via community-led eco-tourism and anti-poaching efforts, spotlighting troops of colobus monkeys and rare owl-faced monkeys, all accessible 4-5 hours from Kigali with stays ranging from luxury lodges like One&Only Nyungwe House to humble guesthouses near Gisakura.

Volcanoes National Park

Volcanoes National Park, a 160-square-kilometer volcanic expanse in northwest Rwanda’s Virunga Massif, is a global icon of mountain gorilla conservation, cradling a third of the world’s 1,000 remaining gorillas amid bamboo forests and five dormant peaks like Karisimbi and Bisoke. Synonymous with Dian Fossey’s legacy, it offers strenuous yet awe-inspiring gorilla treks through muddy trails to witness silverbacks and their families, alongside lighter golden monkey tracking and volcano hikes revealing caves and panoramic views across three countries. With over 130 bird species and rare flora like giant lobelias, the park’s $1,500 trek permits fund protection and local projects, accessible via a 2-hour drive from Kigali to Musanze, where luxury lodges like Bisate and simpler stays cater to adventurers drawn to its raw, misty beauty.

Gishwati-Mukura National Park

Gishwati-Mukura National Park, a 34-square-kilometer forest reserve in northwest Rwanda, is an emerging ecological marvel reborn from deforestation’s scars since its 2015 designation, linking two fragments into a sanctuary of eastern chimpanzees, blue monkeys, and 130+ bird species like the regal crested crane. Less crowded than Nyungwe, it offers intimate chimp treks, guided walks through regenerating landscapes, and birdwatching amid grasslands and wetlands, with community-run tea plantations and eco-lodges enhancing the experience. Rwanda’s reforestation efforts shine here, aiming to unify the forests into a corridor, just 3 hours from Kigali via Nyabihu or Rubavu, where basic lodging like Gishwati Lodge supports a pioneering visit to a wilderness on the mend.

Highlights: Primate Viewing Forest Walks Conservation Tours Birdwatching Eco-Lodges

Location:north-western part of Rwanda, not far from Lake Kivu.

Timings:Anytime during the day.

Kigali’s Natural History Museum

Kigali’s Natural History Museum, housed in the historic Kandt House in the capital’s Nyarugenge district, is an urban haven for wildlife education, blending German colonial architecture with exhibits of Rwanda’s fauna—gorillas, elephants, and endemic birds—through fossils, taxidermy, and interactive displays. A short walk from downtown, this accessible museum offers guided tours tracing the country’s geological and biological past, garden strolls among native plants like flame trees, and hands-on activities for kids, all for a modest $5 entry. While not a wild habitat, it contextualizes Rwanda’s parks with conservation stories like rhino reintroduction, making it a perfect primer or complement for travelers seeking nature insights without leaving the city’s vibrant pulse.

Located: Kigali’s Nyarugenge district, a short walk or drive from downtown.

Ibanda-Makera Forest

Ibanda-Makera Forest, a 200-hectare woodland sanctuary 30 kilometers east of Kigali in Kayonza District, is a serene, lesser-known retreat where birdwatchers and nature lovers escape to find over 150 species—including Albertine Rift endemics like the African pitta—amid dense canopy and babbling streams. Just 45 minutes from the capital, guided walks reveal small mammals like duikers, vibrant sunbirds, and tranquil picnic spots, supported by community efforts to curb encroachment with tourism funds aiding tree planting. Lacking formal facilities, it’s a bring-your-own adventure, offering a peaceful contrast to urban bustle and a convenient add-on to Akagera visits, with local guides enriching the experience of this avian-rich hideaway.

Location: Kayonza District, 30 kilometers east of Kigali (45-minute drive).

Best Time: Tuesday – Sunday, 8:30am to 4: 30pm
